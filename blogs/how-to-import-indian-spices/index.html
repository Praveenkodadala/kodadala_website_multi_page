How to Import Indian Spices: Complete Buyer's Checklist

Step-by-step guide for importers — supplier vetting, quality checks, documentation, and common mistakes to avoid when importing from India.

K
Kodadala TeamSpice Export Specialists · March 5, 2024

Importing Indian spices successfully requires navigating supplier selection, quality verification, documentation requirements, and customs procedures. This step-by-step guide covers everything first-time and experienced importers need to know.

Buyer's Checklist
  1. Finding the Right Supplier
  2. Quality Verification
  3. Required Documentation
  4. Import Regulations
  5. Shipping and Incoterms
  6. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Step 1: Finding the Right Supplier

1

Verify IEC Registration

Every legitimate Indian spice exporter must have an Import Export Code (IEC) from DGFT. Ask for the IEC number and verify it on the DGFT official portal.

2

Check Spice Board Registration

Look for e-RCMC (Registration Cum Membership Certificate) from Spices Board of India. This is mandatory for spice exporters.

3

Request References

Ask for references from buyers in your country or similar markets. A reliable exporter will have testimonials from satisfied customers.

4

Request Samples First

Always request a sample before placing a bulk order. Evaluate colour, aroma, moisture, and lab test results.

Step 2: Quality Verification

Quality verification is the most critical step in importing Indian spices. Substandard spices can result in customs rejection, product recalls, or health risks.

Visual Checks

  • Colour uniformity and vibrancy
  • Free from visible foreign matter
  • Appropriate moisture (not clumping)
  • Characteristic aroma

Lab Tests Required

  • Moisture content
  • Total ash and acid-insoluble ash
  • Pesticide residue (MRL compliance)
  • Aflatoxin B1 and total aflatoxin
  • Microbial count (E.coli, Salmonella)
  • Heavy metals (if required)

Certificates to Request

  • Certificate of Analysis (COA)
  • Phytosanitary Certificate
  • Fumigation Certificate
  • Certificate of Origin

Step 4: Import Regulations by Market

Import regulations vary significantly by destination. Here is a brief overview of the key requirements for major markets:

USA (FDA)

All food imports are subject to FDA Prior Notice. Spices must comply with FDA MRL guidelines and may be subject to Import Alerts if issues are detected. A US agent or importer of record is required.

European Union

EU has strict MRL requirements under EC No 396/2005. Aflatoxin limits are particularly strict. All organic claims must be backed by accredited certification. TRACES notification may be required.

UAE and GCC

GCC standards (GSO) apply. Halal certification may be required for some products. All labels must include Arabic language. Municipality approvals required upon arrival.

Australia

DAFF (formerly DAWE) regulations apply. Mandatory fumigation or heat treatment for most spices. Import permits may be required. Biosecurity compliance is strict.

Step 5: Shipping and Incoterms

For most bulk spice imports, sea freight (FCL or LCL) is the most cost-effective option. FOB (Free on Board) and CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight) are the most common Incoterms used in Indian spice trade.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not verifying supplier certifications before placing order
  • Skipping pre-shipment sample inspection
  • Not checking your country's specific MRL requirements for that spice
  • Accepting a COA without checking the testing lab's NABL accreditation
  • Not specifying moisture and purity thresholds in the purchase order
  • Not arranging fumigation certificate when shipping to USA or Australia
